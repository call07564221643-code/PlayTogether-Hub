A sustainable activity platform for safer inclusive play.

PlayTogether Hub supports schools, managed playgrounds, professionals, parents and caregivers with practical resources for children aged 4–12 from different ethnicities, backgrounds, disabilities, autism spectrum profiles and communication needs.

Business purpose

The platform offers sustainable themed activities and resources for schools, playgrounds managed by professionals, and any setting that wants structured inclusive play.

Who it helps

Professionals, parents and caregivers supporting children who may be autistic, disabled, anxious, multilingual, delayed in communication or at risk of exclusion.

Core promise

Children should gain confidence, enjoy stress-free play, communicate in different ways and avoid being excluded from group activities.

Training material

Short modules for professionals, parents and caregivers

Module 01

Behaviour as Communication

Recognise distress, freezing, masking, withdrawal and avoidance before labelling behaviour as defiance.

Module 02

Autism & Sensory Needs

Plan for sound, light, smell, texture, transitions, instructions, safe exits and calm regulation spaces.

Module 03

Cultural Inclusion

Support children from different ethnicities, languages and backgrounds without embarrassment or tokenism.

Module 04

Non-Verbal Participation

Use cards, gestures, objects, drawing, pointing and role choice so speech is not the only route.

Module 05

Safeguarding in Play

Identify peer pressure, humiliation, unsafe dynamics, adult boundaries and when to stop an activity.

Module 06

Adult Scripts

Use calm language such as “You can pass”, “Show me another way”, and “Let’s make it smaller”.

Safeguarding

Safeguarding must be visible before, during and after play

This project is educational and should be aligned with the setting’s safeguarding policy, DSL procedures, Ofsted expectations and local authority routes.

Before starting

  • Check known sensory triggers, medical needs and mobility access.
  • Prepare visual choices, pass cards and a quiet exit option.
  • Agree adult roles and who observes safety, not just activity completion.

During play

  • Never force speech, eye contact, touch, speed or public performance.
  • Watch for freezing, hiding, silence, distress, sudden anger or withdrawal.
  • Stop if peers laugh, dominate, exclude or shame a child.

What adults should be aware of

  • Sensory issues: noise, smell, light, texture, crowding and transitions.
  • Anxiety: unexpected changes, unclear rules, competitive pressure or fear of mistakes.
  • Communication: some children use movement, pointing, silence, objects or avoidance.

After the activity

  • Record what helped the child participate safely.
  • Review if group size, timing or materials need adjusting.
  • Escalate concerns through the correct safeguarding route if needed.
